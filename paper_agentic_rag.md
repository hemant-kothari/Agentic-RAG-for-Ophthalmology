## Abstract

The integration of artificial intelligence into clinical ophthalmology has yielded remarkable progress in automated image interpretation, yet a persistent gap remains between the act of detection and the generation of actionable, evidence-grounded clinical knowledge. This paper presents a domain-constrained Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system designed to bridge that gap by converting the probabilistic outputs of a deep learning retinal image classifier into structured, guideline-referenced clinical context. The proposed system operates in two distinct interaction modes: an image-driven mode that processes inference outputs from a Vision Transformer model and generates per-disease clinical summaries, and an open-ended query mode that enables clinicians and researchers to interrogate a curated multi-source knowledge base spanning eight international ophthalmic organisations. The knowledge base comprises 2,163 semantically indexed document chunks derived from 92 clinical guidelines and review papers, encoded with a bi-encoder sentence embedding model and indexed using a Flat Inner Product FAISS structure. A multi-tier guardrail mechanism governs all system outputs, enforcing scope boundaries, sanitising inputs, and appending mandatory clinical disclaimers. When knowledge base retrieval confidence falls below a defined threshold, the system transparently augments its context with web search results, clearly distinguishing supplementary material from peer-reviewed guideline evidence. Empirical evaluation across six clinical query categories demonstrates successful multi-source retrieval across all test cases, with responses drawing coherently from NICE, AAO, WHO, EURETINA, RCOphth, ADA, and India Vision 2025 sources simultaneously. The paper concludes with a forward-looking discussion on evolving this architecture toward a persistent, self-updating knowledge management system inspired by the LLM-Wiki paradigm.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Motivation and Clinical Context

Ophthalmic diseases constitute a globally significant burden of preventable vision loss. Conditions such as diabetic retinopathy, age-related macular degeneration, primary open-angle glaucoma, and hypertensive retinopathy collectively affect hundreds of millions of people worldwide. What makes these conditions particularly amenable to artificial intelligence is their shared dependency on retinal imaging: the fundus photograph, optical coherence tomography scan, and fluorescein angiogram offer rich visual data from which trained neural networks can extract pathological patterns with high diagnostic accuracy.

The past decade has witnessed the rise of deep learning systems capable of matching or exceeding specialist-level performance in retinal disease detection. Models trained on large fundus image repositories can reliably identify the microaneurysms of early diabetic retinopathy, the drusen deposits characteristic of AMD, and the optic disc changes associated with glaucoma. However, the clinical value of a detection result is not realised at the moment of classification alone. A diagnosis, whether generated by a human clinician or a machine learning model, must be situated within a body of clinical knowledge: What are the guidelines for monitoring this condition? What treatment options does the evidence support? What follow-up intervals are recommended at this severity level? These questions cannot be answered by a classification model, no matter how accurate its predictions.

This gap between detection and clinical knowledge synthesis represents a significant unmet need. Clinicians deploying AI tools in screening programmes must still manually consult guidelines, synthesise evidence from multiple sources, and formulate appropriate management plans. For resource-constrained settings — particularly in emerging economies where specialist ophthalmologist availability is limited — this additional cognitive burden can reduce the practical benefit of AI-assisted screening.

### 1.2 Retrieval-Augmented Generation as a Solution Paradigm

Retrieval-Augmented Generation offers a compelling architectural response to this need. By pairing a neural language model with a dense vector retrieval system over a curated knowledge corpus, RAG systems can generate responses that are both linguistically fluent and factually grounded in specific source documents. Unlike purely generative models that rely on parametric knowledge encoded during pre-training, RAG systems retrieve relevant passages at inference time, reducing hallucination and enabling transparent citation of sources.

In a clinical context, the properties of RAG are especially desirable. The ability to ground every clinical claim in a citable guideline excerpt addresses the fundamental trustworthiness requirement of medical AI: clinicians and patients must be able to trace the reasoning behind any recommendation to a documented, authoritative source. Furthermore, RAG systems can be updated independently of their language model by modifying the knowledge base, allowing timely incorporation of new guidelines without the prohibitive cost of model retraining.

### 1.3 The Agentic Extension

Standard RAG architectures are reactive: they receive a query, retrieve relevant passages, and generate a response. Agentic RAG extends this paradigm by introducing a planning layer that can decompose complex inputs into multiple targeted retrieval operations, assess the adequacy of retrieved evidence, dynamically route to alternative information sources when necessary, and enforce structured output formats appropriate to the context.

The system presented in this paper adopts an agentic design specifically because its primary input — the structured JSON output of a multi-label Vision Transformer — is not a natural language query but a complex structured prediction containing multiple disease labels, associated confidence scores, severity grades, and attention statistics. Converting this structured input into an effective retrieval strategy requires an orchestration layer capable of mapping detected conditions to domain-appropriate query formulations, aggregating evidence across multiple retrieval passes, and synthesising a coherent multi-disease clinical report.

### 1.4 Contributions

The principal contributions of this work are as follows:

1. **A heterogeneous clinical knowledge base** comprising 2,163 semantically indexed chunks drawn from 92 source documents across eight international ophthalmic organisations, constructed using a novel hybrid parser pipeline that handles diverse document formats including structured policy guidelines, journal review papers, and two-column academic publications.

2. **An agentic orchestration framework** that processes both structured Vision Transformer outputs (image mode) and free-text clinical queries (query mode) through a unified retrieval, evaluation, and generation pipeline.

3. **A multi-tier guardrail system** that enforces clinical scope boundaries, sanitises inputs against prompt injection, applies certainty-based confidence labelling to AI-detected findings, and appends mandatory clinical disclaimers to all outputs.

4. **A transparent web search fallback mechanism** that activates when knowledge base retrieval confidence is insufficient, clearly distinguishing supplementary web-sourced material from peer-reviewed guideline evidence.

5. **Validated end-to-end performance** across image mode and query mode operation, demonstrating consistent multi-source retrieval and clinically appropriate response generation.

## 2. Literature Survey

### 2.1 Foundations of Retrieval-Augmented Generation

The formal articulation of Retrieval-Augmented Generation as a unified framework emerged from work on open-domain question answering, where the challenge of answering factual questions without access to a fixed knowledge base motivated the combination of parametric (model-encoded) and non-parametric (retrieved) knowledge. Early neural retrieval systems employed sparse term-matching approaches inherited from traditional information retrieval, but the development of bi-encoder dense retrieval models introduced a fundamentally different paradigm: queries and documents are mapped into a shared dense vector space, and semantic similarity is computed as inner product or cosine distance rather than term overlap. This shift enabled retrieval of contextually relevant passages even when they share no vocabulary with the query.

Subsequent work explored the integration of dense retrieval with sequence-to-sequence language models, establishing the RAG architecture as a principled end-to-end trainable system. These systems demonstrated that conditioning generation on dynamically retrieved passages significantly reduced factual errors compared to purely generative models, particularly for knowledge-intensive tasks where the answer depended on specific documented facts rather than distributional regularities in training data.

### 2.2 Clinical and Biomedical Applications of RAG

The biomedical domain has emerged as a natural testbed for RAG systems due to the combination of high factual precision requirements and the existence of well-structured, authoritative knowledge repositories. Clinical decision support systems have historically relied on rule-based expert systems or curated medical ontologies, but the emergence of large language models created interest in more flexible, natural-language-driven interfaces to clinical knowledge.

Subsequent research in clinical RAG explored the specific challenges of biomedical text retrieval, including the density of domain-specific terminology, the importance of distinguishing between different levels of evidence (randomised controlled trials versus expert consensus versus case reports), and the safety-critical requirement that clinical responses not misrepresent or extrapolate beyond documented evidence. These investigations informed design choices around retrieval granularity, context window management, and output format constraints that are directly relevant to the present work.

Medical dialogue systems incorporating retrieval have demonstrated that grounding responses in source documents substantially improves clinician trust compared to purely generative responses, even when the linguistic quality of retrieved-grounded responses is slightly lower. This finding supports the design philosophy of the present system, which prioritises citation transparency over linguistic polish.

### 2.3 Ophthalmic Artificial Intelligence

The application of deep learning to retinal image analysis has a well-established research trajectory. Convolutional neural networks trained on large fundus photograph datasets demonstrated screening-level diabetic retinopathy detection performance, prompting regulatory approval and commercial deployment of AI screening tools in multiple countries. More recently, Vision Transformer architectures pre-trained on large ophthalmology-specific corpora have achieved state-of-the-art results across multiple concurrent retinal disease detection tasks, including the simultaneous screening for diabetic retinopathy, age-related macular degeneration, glaucoma, and other conditions from a single fundus image.

However, published ophthalmic AI systems have predominantly focused on classification performance metrics without addressing the downstream need for guideline-grounded clinical context generation. The integration of ophthalmic AI classifiers with clinical knowledge retrieval systems remains an underexplored area, and no published work known to the authors addresses the specific challenge of converting multi-label, multi-confidence retinal image analysis outputs into structured, multi-source, citation-backed clinical guidance reports.

### 2.4 Agentic AI Systems

The concept of agentic AI — systems that can plan, take sequential actions, observe outcomes, and adapt their behaviour accordingly — has been explored extensively in the context of tool-using language models. Frameworks such as ReAct introduced the idea of interleaving chain-of-thought reasoning with external tool calls, enabling language models to retrieve information, execute code, and interact with APIs as part of a coherent reasoning process.

In the medical context, agentic systems introduce both opportunity and risk. The ability to decompose complex clinical questions into targeted sub-queries, evaluate the adequacy of retrieved evidence, and dynamically augment with alternative sources has clear potential to improve response comprehensiveness. Simultaneously, the autonomous action-taking characteristic of agentic systems demands robust guardrail mechanisms to prevent the generation of clinically harmful content, enforce scope limitations, and maintain clear attribution of information sources.

### 2.5 Clinical Guardrails and Safety Constraints

The deployment of language models in clinical settings has prompted significant interest in safety constraint mechanisms. Input sanitisation to prevent adversarial prompt injection, output filtering to remove inappropriate medical claims, and mandatory disclaimer injection represent the primary categories of clinical guardrail implementations. Research in medical AI ethics has emphasised that the transparency of confidence labelling — how clearly a system communicates the uncertainty associated with its outputs — is as important as the accuracy of those outputs in determining safe deployment characteristics.

The present work adopts a certainty-threshold approach to confidence labelling, translating continuous model certainty scores into categorical labels (Confirmed versus Possible) that communicate the appropriate level of clinical caution without exposing raw numerical probabilities that may be misinterpreted by non-specialist users.

## 3. Theoretical Background

### 3.1 Dense Bi-Encoder Retrieval

The retrieval component of the present system is built on the dense bi-encoder paradigm. A bi-encoder model consists of two independent encoder networks — one for queries and one for documents — that are trained jointly such that the inner product of their output vectors reflects semantic relevance. At indexing time, all document passages are encoded once and stored as dense vectors. At query time, the query is encoded and the nearest document vectors are found through approximate nearest neighbour search.

The choice of inner product similarity (as opposed to cosine similarity or Euclidean distance) is aligned with the normalisation strategy applied to both query and document vectors. When embeddings are L2-normalised to unit length before indexing and retrieval, the inner product is mathematically equivalent to cosine similarity, combining the directional interpretation of cosine distance with the computational efficiency of inner product search.

The embedding model employed in this work is `sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2`, a lightweight distilled transformer that maps variable-length text sequences to 384-dimensional dense vectors. This model was selected for its strong balance of semantic retrieval quality and computational efficiency, making it suitable for the repeated inference operations required during multi-query retrieval in image mode.

### 3.2 FAISS Indexing

Facebook AI Similarity Search provides the index structure underlying the retrieval component. The system employs a `IndexFlatIP` index, which performs exact exhaustive search over all stored vectors using inner product as the similarity metric. While approximate search methods offer superior scalability for very large corpora, the exact index is preferred for the present knowledge base of 2,163 vectors, where the search overhead is negligible and the guarantee of exact top-k retrieval eliminates any accuracy degradation from approximation.

The FAISS index is complemented by two parallel data structures: a metadata store (serialised as a Python pickle file) containing structured information about each chunk including guideline identifier, source organisation, section title, disease category, and publication year; and a chunk text store (in JSON Lines format) containing the raw passage text. This three-file architecture was designed for incremental append operations, enabling new knowledge sources to be incorporated without full re-indexing.

### 3.3 Chunking Strategy

Document passages are decomposed into fixed-size overlapping chunks of approximately 400 words with a 50-word overlap between consecutive chunks. This chunking strategy reflects a balance between retrieval granularity and context density. Chunks that are too short may lack sufficient context for the language model to generate grounded responses; chunks that are too long risk exceeding the effective context window and introducing off-topic material that degrades response quality.

The 50-word overlap between consecutive chunks preserves semantic continuity across chunk boundaries, ensuring that sentences or clinical recommendations that straddle a chunk boundary are represented in at least one complete chunk.

### 3.4 Certainty-Threshold Confidence Labelling

A critical design decision in the system is the mapping from continuous model certainty scores to categorical confidence labels. The Vision Transformer model produces two distinct numerical outputs for each detected condition: a classification probability (the sigmoid-activated output of the binary classification head) and a certainty score (a measure of the model's confidence in its own prediction, derived from prediction margin or entropy-based calibration).

The system applies a certainty threshold of 0.15: findings with a certainty score at or above this threshold are labelled as Confirmed, while findings below the threshold are labelled as Possible — Flagged for Clinical Review. This threshold was determined empirically to capture the meaningful distinction between high-confidence detections (where the model's internal evidence is strong and consistent) and uncertain positive predictions (where the probability crosses the decision boundary but the model's internal state reflects ambiguity). In the example inference result analysed in this paper, Hypertensive Retinopathy with a certainty of 0.573 is classified as Confirmed, while AMD with a certainty of 0.031 is classified as Possible, reflecting the model's own assessment of its prediction reliability.

### 3.5 Retrieval Relevance Assessment and Web Fallback

The system assesses the quality of knowledge base retrieval by examining the maximum cosine similarity score achieved among the top-k retrieved chunks. When this maximum score falls below a configured threshold of 0.35, the system infers that the knowledge base contains insufficient directly relevant material for the query. In this case, a web search is performed using DuckDuckGo's text search API, with the query prefixed by "ophthalmology clinical guideline" to bias results toward peer-reviewed and professional sources.

Web search results are incorporated into the context window with explicit labelling distinguishing them from knowledge base chunks. The language model is instructed in its system prompt to clearly identify when a statement is sourced from web results rather than indexed guidelines, maintaining the transparency and auditability of the system's reasoning.

## 4. Methodology

### 4.1 Knowledge Base Construction

The construction of a comprehensive, multi-source ophthalmic knowledge base required the systematic processing of 92 PDF documents from nine source categories. The source collection spans NICE (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence) from the United Kingdom; the American Academy of Ophthalmology (AAO); the American Diabetes Association (ADA); the Royal College of Ophthalmologists (RCOphth); the European Society of Retina Specialists (EURETINA); the World Health Organization (WHO); the International Council of Ophthalmology; India Vision 2025 initiative; and a curated selection of peer-reviewed systematic reviews and clinical practice review papers addressing diabetic retinopathy, age-related macular degeneration, glaucoma, and hypertensive retinopathy.

The 72 NICE documents, comprising guidance on ophthalmic procedures, technology appraisals, and clinical guidelines, were processed in an initial phase using a structured extraction pipeline. The additional 20 documents from international organisations constituted the second phase, processed using the hybrid parser pipeline described in Section 4.2.

#### 4.1.1 Format Analysis and Parser Assignment

A systematic pre-processing inspection step was implemented to extract and review the first 120 lines of each PDF document's extracted text, revealing the structural conventions of each source. Three distinct format categories were identified:

**Category A: Hierarchical Policy Guidelines** — Documents from the AAO, formatted with consistent ALL-CAPS or Title-Case section headings appearing as standalone lines, with numbered subsection hierarchies and extensive cross-referencing. Four AAO Preferred Practice Pattern documents (AMD, DR, POAG, and suspect POAG management) fell into this category.

**Category B: Numbered Sequential Guidelines** — Documents from RCOphth, WHO, and India Vision 2025, employing numerically prefixed section headings (1., 2.1, 2.1.1) with relatively uniform single-column layouts. Three RCOphth documents, one WHO document, and the India Vision 2025 framework fell into this category.

**Category C: Academic and Two-Column Publications** — Documents from EURETINA, ADA, and review journals, typically formatted with abstract-introduction-methods-results-discussion structure or as two-column journal layouts. Six review papers and three organisational summaries fell into this category.

#### 4.1.2 Hybrid Parser Design

Three specialised parsers were implemented to handle the identified format categories:

The **AAO PPP Parser** employs a line-by-line scanning approach that identifies section boundaries through heading detection logic combining capitalisation patterns and contextual position. Text below each identified heading is collected and stored as a section object with associated metadata.

The **Society Guideline Parser** targets numbered section hierarchies using regular expression matching for numeral-prefixed titles (patterns such as `^[0-9]+\.[0-9.]*\s+[^\d]`). A critical safety mechanism filters lines that match the heading pattern but consist entirely of a title followed by a page reference numeral — a pattern that arises in table-of-contents pages and would otherwise cause spurious section splits. A minimum content length threshold of 200 characters prevents section objects from being created for navigational text.

The **Academic Review Parser** uses a keyword-based heading detection strategy targeting standard academic section labels (Abstract, Introduction, Background, Methods, Results, Discussion, Conclusion, References, Treatment, Management, Screening, Diagnosis). For two-column journal PDFs where pdfplumber's text extraction collapses column boundaries and disrupts heading-line detection, a paragraph-based fallback chunking strategy is employed that groups consecutive non-empty paragraphs until a 600-word threshold is reached.

#### 4.1.3 Section Metadata Enrichment

Each extracted section is stored as a JSON object with the following schema:

```json
{
  "guideline_id": "RCOphth_AMD_2024",
  "title": "AMD Services Evidence Base 2024",
  "source": "Royal College of Ophthalmologists",
  "source_org": "RCOphth",
  "source_type": "Commissioning Guidance",
  "disease": "Age-Related Macular Degeneration",
  "year": 2024,
  "sections": [
    {
      "section_title": "Anti-VEGF Treatment Pathways",
      "section_type": "Recommendations",
      "content": "..."
    }
  ]
}
```

The `source_org` and `disease` fields, absent from the original NICE-phase metadata, enable downstream retrieval filtering by organisation or condition.

### 4.2 Embedding and Vector Database Construction

Each extracted section's content is chunked into passages of approximately 400 words with 50-word overlap. Chunked passages are encoded using the `all-MiniLM-L6-v2` sentence transformer model to produce 384-dimensional dense vectors. Vectors are L2-normalised prior to FAISS indexing.

The FAISS `IndexFlatIP` index is maintained alongside two companion files: a pickle-serialised list of metadata dictionaries (one per chunk) and a JSONL file of chunk texts. The three-file structure supports incremental appends through the `append_to_vdb.py` utility, which loads the existing index, encodes new chunks, appends vectors to the index, and updates all three files atomically.

### 4.3 Agentic System Architecture

The agentic system is implemented as a Python package (`agentic_rag`) containing the following principal modules:

- **`agent.py`**: The central orchestrator implementing the `RetinalRAGAgent` class with `run_image_mode()` and `run_query_mode()` public interfaces.
- **`retriever.py`**: The FAISS retrieval interface, loaded once at module import and providing a `retrieve()` function supporting optional filtering by source organisation, disease category, and minimum similarity score.
- **`llm_client.py`**: A Groq API wrapper implementing automatic key rotation across multiple API credentials and configurable retry logic for rate-limit handling.
- **`input_parser.py`**: The Vision Transformer output parser implementing the certainty-threshold confidence labelling logic.
- **`guardrails.py`**: The safety enforcement module implementing off-topic detection, input sanitisation, and disclaimer injection.
- **`web_search.py`**: The DuckDuckGo search fallback implementation with medical query prefix bias.
- **`context_builder.py`**: The context assembly module handling KB chunk and web result merging, deduplication, and token budget enforcement.

The system is exposed through two interfaces: a command-line application (`run_rag.py`) supporting image mode, single-query mode, and interactive session mode; and a Gradio web application (`rag_app.py`) providing a two-tab browser interface.

## 5. System Working

### 5.1 Image Mode Pipeline

When the system operates in image mode, it receives the path to an `inference_results.json` file produced by the upstream Vision Transformer model. This file contains a ranked list of condition predictions with associated probabilities, certainty scores, a suppression flag for the normal retina class, a DR severity grade from the CORAL ordinal regression head, and a 512-dimensional embedding of the image representation.

**Step 1 — Input Parsing**: The `input_parser` module reads the JSON file and partitions the detected conditions into Confirmed findings (certainty ≥ 0.15) and Possible findings (certainty < 0.15, but positive prediction above the 0.5 decision threshold). Human-readable labels replace raw numerical values: probabilities are mapped to High/Moderate/Low confidence categories, and certainty scores are mapped to descriptive phrases (e.g., "Low certainty — flagged for clinical review").

**Step 2 — Per-Disease Query Construction**: For each detected finding (both Confirmed and Possible), the agent selects a pre-configured set of two to three retrieval queries from a disease-to-query mapping table. For example, a detection of Hypertensive Retinopathy triggers queries including "hypertensive retinopathy classification grading management", "blood pressure retinal damage treatment monitoring", and "hypertensive retinopathy screening follow-up guidelines". This multi-query strategy ensures that the retrieved context covers the range of clinically relevant aspects — classification, treatment, monitoring — for each condition rather than fixating on a single aspect.

**Step 3 — Retrieval**: Each query is independently submitted to the FAISS retrieval system, which returns up to 8 chunks per query with similarity scores above the 0.30 minimum threshold. Results from all queries across all findings are pooled and deduplicated by comparing the first 80 characters of each chunk's text.

**Step 4 — Relevance Assessment**: The maximum similarity score across all retrieved chunks is evaluated. If this value falls below 0.35, indicating that the knowledge base contains no strongly relevant material, the web search fallback is activated for a combined query formed from the condition names.

**Step 5 — Context Assembly**: The `context_builder` module merges knowledge base chunks and optional web results into a single formatted context string, applying a 3,000-word budget cap. Each KB chunk is labelled with its source organisation and guideline identifier. Web results are labelled with explicit markers distinguishing them as supplementary material.

**Step 6 — LLM Generation**: The assembled context, alongside a structured representation of the detected findings, is submitted to the Groq-hosted Llama 3.3 70B Versatile model through the `LLMClient` interface. The system prompt instructs the model to generate a structured clinical report with separate subsections for each finding, citing guideline identifiers for each clinical statement and maintaining cautious clinical language throughout. The maximum generation length is set to 1,500 tokens.

**Step 7 — Output Formatting**: A mandatory clinical disclaimer is appended to every response before it is returned to the caller, regardless of the content of the generated text. This ensures that the safety boundary is enforced at the infrastructure level rather than relying on the language model to self-apply the disclaimer consistently.

### 5.2 Query Mode Pipeline

When operating in query mode, the system receives a free-text clinical question from a human user. The pipeline follows a similar structure to image mode but begins with two additional safety gates.

**Input Sanitisation**: The query is checked against a set of regular expression patterns targeting known prompt injection attempts — phrases such as "ignore previous instructions", "act as", "jailbreak", and "system prompt". Detected injection attempts result in the query being rejected with an informative error message. Additionally, queries exceeding 2,000 characters are truncated to prevent token budget overflows.

**Off-Topic Detection**: The sanitised query is evaluated against a keyword-based off-topic detection system. The system maintains a list of in-scope keywords covering all diseases, anatomical structures, diagnostic procedures, treatment modalities, and clinical processes covered by the knowledge base. If none of these keywords are present in the query, a set of off-topic pattern matching rules is applied targeting categories such as food, entertainment, finance, general medicine (non-ophthalmic), and unrelated technology topics. Queries matching off-topic patterns are responded to with a polite scope clarification message listing the system's competency areas, without invoking the language model.

If the query passes both gates, retrieval proceeds against the full knowledge base with a top-k of 10 chunks. The web fallback logic, context building, LLM generation, and disclaimer injection proceed identically to image mode.

### 5.3 Guardrail System Summary

The system enforces five distinct guardrail mechanisms:

| Guardrail | Mechanism | Scope |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| Prompt injection sanitisation | Regex pattern matching | Input |
| Off-topic rejection | Keyword + regex classification | Input |
| Certainty labelling | Threshold-based categorical mapping | Input parsing |
| Web result labelling | Explicit context markers | Context |
| Clinical disclaimer | Mandatory post-generation append | Output |

## 6. Results and Evaluation

### 6.1 Knowledge Base Quality

The final knowledge base of 2,163 vectors spans a representative cross-section of contemporary international ophthalmic guidance. Source diversity is substantial: the largest single contributor (NICE) accounts for 45.1% of the total chunk count, with the remaining 54.9% distributed across seven additional organisations. This distribution reflects the deliberate curation strategy of prioritising depth in one well-structured source (NICE's 72 guidance documents) while achieving breadth through targeted inclusion of specialist international sources.

The semantic coverage of the knowledge base was assessed through a set of representative clinical queries formulated to probe different disease areas, treatment modalities, and management aspects. Cosine similarity scores for in-domain queries consistently exceeded 0.45, with peak scores for highly specific clinical topics reaching 0.65–0.75, confirming that the sentence embedding model and chunking strategy successfully capture the semantic structure of the clinical domain.

### 6.2 Retrieval Validation

A formal six-query retrieval validation was conducted upon completion of knowledge base construction. Each query was formulated to probe a distinct combination of disease area and query type, and expected source organisations were identified for each query based on known content:

| Query | Expected Orgs | Found Orgs | Status |
|-------|--------------|------------|--------|
| HbA1c targets and DR progression | ADA, WHO, EURETINA, AAO, RCOphth | AAO, NICE, RCOphth | PASS |
| Anti-VEGF for diabetic macular oedema | AAO, EURETINA, RCOphth, ADA | AAO, NICE, RCOphth, WHO | PASS |
| Hypertensive retinopathy (Keith-Wagener) | REVIEW | REVIEW | PASS |
| IOP targets in glaucoma | AAO, RCOphth | AAO, RCOphth, REVIEW | PASS |
| DR screening in India | INDIA, WHO | AAO, INDIA, WHO | PASS |
| Faricimab/brolucizumab AMD treatment | AAO, RCOphth, EURETINA | AAO, NICE, RCOphth | PASS |

All six test queries achieved the pass criterion of returning at least one chunk from an expected source organisation. Notably, most queries returned results from multiple organisations, confirming that the unified multi-source index enables cross-organisational evidence synthesis — a key objective of the knowledge base design.

### 6.3 Image Mode Evaluation

The image mode pipeline was evaluated using the inference output file produced by the upstream Vision Transformer for a sample fundus image. The inference result contained two positive predictions:

- **Hypertensive Retinopathy**: probability 0.787, certainty 0.573 → Labelled CONFIRMED (High confidence, High certainty)
- **Age-Related Macular Degeneration**: probability 0.516, certainty 0.031 → Labelled POSSIBLE — Flagged for Clinical Review (Low confidence, Low certainty)

The DR severity CORAL head predicted Grade 0 (No DR) with a grade certainty of 0.997, indicating high confidence in the absence of diabetic retinopathy.

The system retrieved evidence from 11 distinct guideline sources covering both conditions, including AAO_AMD_PPP_2024, EURETINA_AMD_2023, RCOphth_AMD_2024, REVIEW_HTN_RET_CLINICAL, REVIEW_SURV_OPHTH_2026, and several NICE technology appraisals. The generated clinical report correctly:

- Distinguished between the two findings using the Confirmed/Possible labelling
- Cited different guideline sources for statements about hypertensive retinopathy versus AMD
- Noted the relevance of new imaging technologies (OCT, OCTA) for hypertensive retinopathy assessment
- Referenced AREDS/AREDS2 supplementation evidence for the AMD finding
- Appended the clinical disclaimer without modification

### 6.4 Query Mode Evaluation

The query mode was evaluated using a representative in-scope clinical question: "What are the recommended screening intervals for diabetic retinopathy?" The system retrieved three relevant chunks from NICE (NG242), WHO (WHO_DR_SCREENING_2020), and RCOphth (RCOphth_DR_2024), and generated a structured response correctly summarising:

- The WHO recommendation for one-to-two-year rescreening intervals
- The NICE NG242 guidance distinguishing between 6–12 month intervals for moderate non-proliferative DR and 3-month intervals for severe/very severe non-proliferative DR
- The RCOphth perspective on screening commencement age criteria

The guardrail evaluation confirmed that the off-topic detection system correctly rejected the query "What is the best restaurant in Mumbai?" with the scope clarification response, without invoking the language model.

### 6.5 Qualitative Observations

Several qualitative characteristics of the system's outputs merit observation. The language model consistently adhered to the citation instruction, typically including the source organisation name and guideline identifier parenthetically within the relevant sentence rather than only in a reference list. This inline citation approach is clinically useful as it allows readers to assess the provenance of individual claims without consulting a separate reference section.

The system's multi-source context assembly produced responses that reflected the moderate variation in guidance across organisations — for example, differences in anti-VEGF agent preferences between AAO, EURETINA, and NICE sources — rather than artificially homogenising conflicting recommendations. This preservation of inter-organisational variation is a meaningful feature for clinical users who require awareness of international consensus versus national guideline divergence.

## 7. Future Scope

### 7.1 Limitations of the Static Knowledge Base Paradigm

The architecture presented in this paper treats the knowledge base as a static artifact: constructed once, validated, and deployed without modification until a human administrator decides to add new source documents and re-run the append pipeline. This approach is appropriate for a research prototype but introduces meaningful limitations in a deployment context. Clinical guidelines are revised regularly — sometimes annually, as with NICE technology appraisals — and the evidence landscape for emerging treatments such as gene therapy for inherited retinal dystrophies or sustained-release anti-VEGF implants for AMD evolves rapidly. A static knowledge base will inevitably develop gaps between its indexed knowledge and the current state of clinical evidence.

Furthermore, the current architecture does not retain any memory of previous interactions. Each query or image analysis request is processed entirely independently, with no mechanism for the system to build on insights from previous sessions, recognise recurring knowledge gaps, or consolidate related evidence encountered across multiple queries into structured summaries.

### 7.2 The LLM-Wiki Paradigm

A promising direction for addressing these limitations draws inspiration from the concept of the LLM-Wiki: a dynamic, collaboratively maintained knowledge structure in which a language model participates not only as a retrieval-conditioned response generator but also as an active contributor to the maintenance and evolution of the knowledge base itself. In the LLM-Wiki paradigm, retrieved information is not discarded after response generation but is instead evaluated, synthesised, and potentially stored as a new knowledge artifact — a structured summary, a reconciliation note, or an updated factual entry — that enriches the knowledge base for future queries.

Applied to the ophthalmic RAG system, this paradigm suggests an architecture in which:

**Synthesis Persistence**: When a query triggers the retrieval of related but non-identical evidence from multiple guidelines — for example, slightly different monitoring interval recommendations from NICE, AAO, and RCOphth for the same condition — the system could generate and store a structured synthesis artifact noting the areas of consensus and divergence. Subsequent queries on the same topic would benefit from this pre-computed synthesis, improving both response quality and retrieval efficiency.

**Gap Detection and Flagging**: When the web search fallback is triggered, indicating that the knowledge base lacks sufficient coverage of a topic, this event could be logged as a structured knowledge gap record, enabling administrators to prioritise knowledge base expansion toward underrepresented areas. Over time, the accumulation of gap records provides a data-driven basis for knowledge base curation decisions.

**Evidence Linking**: Clinical knowledge about a condition spans multiple guideline documents, review papers, and clinical trial summaries. A persistent knowledge management layer could maintain a graph structure in which knowledge artifacts are linked by disease, treatment modality, or clinical parameter, enabling graph-traversal-based retrieval that complements the existing vector similarity search. For instance, a query about anti-VEGF resistance in AMD could traverse the graph from the neovascular AMD treatment node to linked nodes representing clinical trial data, monitoring protocol guidance, and treatment switching criteria.

### 7.3 Cumulative Learning and Knowledge Refinement

An extended architecture incorporating cumulative learning would maintain a record of which retrieved chunks were most frequently associated with high-quality, positively-evaluated responses, and would use this signal to dynamically re-weight retrieval candidates. Over successive interaction cycles, the retrieval system would develop an implicit understanding of which passages are most clinically actionable for specific query types — not through model retraining, but through accumulated retrieval feedback signals.

This refinement process could also incorporate clinician feedback. If the system is deployed with an optional clinician review interface, structured feedback — for example, a clinician annotating that a particular guideline excerpt is outdated or that the system missed a critical aspect of a condition's management — could be incorporated into the knowledge base through both flagging (tagging the original chunk as requiring review) and augmentation (adding a correction artifact linked to the original chunk).

### 7.4 Multimodal Knowledge Integration

The present system processes image analysis outputs as structured text (the JSON inference result) rather than as raw image data. A natural extension would integrate direct image understanding into the RAG pipeline, enabling the system to retrieve guideline figures, fundus photograph examples from educational resources, and OCT scan reference images as part of its context. This multimodal retrieval capability would enrich the generated reports with visual reference material and enable more nuanced comparison between the analysed image and documented phenotypic patterns.

### 7.5 Federated Knowledge Base Architecture

The centralised knowledge base architecture, while straightforward to implement and maintain, creates a single point of failure and limits adaptability to regional clinical contexts. A federated architecture would maintain separate regional knowledge base partitions — for example, separate sub-indices for UK NICE guidance, US AAO guidance, and India Vision 2025 guidance — with a meta-retrieval layer that selects the most contextually appropriate partition based on the user's institutional affiliation or the detected condition's demographic relevance. This would enable the system to prioritise locally applicable guidelines while retaining access to the full international evidence base when regional guidance is insufficient.

## 8. Conclusion

This paper has presented a domain-constrained Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation system designed to provide evidence-grounded clinical context for ophthalmic disease management. The system addresses the gap between AI-driven retinal image classification and clinically actionable knowledge by integrating a 2,163-vector multi-source knowledge base with an agentic orchestration layer capable of processing structured Vision Transformer outputs, executing multi-query retrieval strategies, assessing evidence adequacy, and generating structured, cited clinical reports.

The multi-tier guardrail architecture ensures that all outputs remain within defined clinical scope boundaries, that AI-detected findings are communicated with appropriate certainty-calibrated labels rather than raw numerical probabilities, and that mandatory clinical disclaimers accompany every response. Validation across image mode and query mode demonstrates consistent multi-source retrieval with responses coherently integrating evidence from internationally authoritative guideline bodies.

The future trajectory of this work, oriented toward persistent knowledge management inspired by the LLM-Wiki paradigm, envisions a system that progressively consolidates clinical knowledge, identifies and addresses its own gaps, and evolves with the clinical evidence landscape — moving from a static retrieval tool toward a living, reasoning knowledge partner for ophthalmic clinical practice.
